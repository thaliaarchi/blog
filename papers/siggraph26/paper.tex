\section{Tape Recovery}

This talk is on UNIX V4 and our recent recovery of it from a magnetic tape.

I'm a PhD student at the University of Utah, studying computer science and
verifying compilers.

While reviewing old documents in preparation for the move from the Merrill
Engineering Building to the new computer science building, Aleks Maricq found a
magnetic tape labeled "UNIX V4".

The UNIX tape was found among the items of Jay Lepreau, an operating systems
researcher who worked with UNIX throughout his career. His first project with
UNIX was during his undergrad, building a virtualized version of UNIX for
TOPS-20 on the PDP-10. Porting the C compiler was complicated by the
architecture having 36-bit words, not divisible by 8 like the PDP-11 that UNIX
grew up on. Lepreau was on the program committee for several Usenix conferences
and founded Operating Systems Design and Implementation.

\section{UNIX History}

UNIX is an influential time-sharing operating system created by Ken Thompson,
Dennis Ritchie, and others at Bell Laboratories in the 1970s. After the shutdown
of the ambitious Multics project, Thompson started work on an operating system
for a disused PDP-7 computer. The limitations of this small machine drove the
innovations of UNIX, by necessity. UNIX distilled the design of Multics to its
necessary core. Soon its potential was recognized by Doug McIlroy and they
secured funding to develop a word processing system for the Patent department.
With these funds, they acquired a new PDP-11 machine.
